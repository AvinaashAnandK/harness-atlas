The loud incident

An agent was asked to find a vision model for a task. It reached for one with total confidence: torch.hub.load('pytorch/vision', 'BerryPicker')

BerryPicker has never existed. The call fired, and failed, the instant it ran.

Your coding agent does this too. Asked to run a database migration, never shown that a migrate tool exists, it invents one. The error arrives before you finish reading the command.

Source: Gorilla, Reality BytesUC Berkeley's Gorilla project, first to measure API hallucination
Label it

The failure announced itself. It is right there in the trace, at the moment it happened, impossible to miss.

Call this kind loud.

The silent incident

An AI-safety director at Meta connected an agent to her inbox. Her instruction was explicit: "don't action until I tell you to."

The inbox was large. Processing it filled the agent's memory, so the agent did what agents do under pressure: it compressed its own record of the conversation to make room.

The instruction did not survive the compression.

The agent began deleting. Over 200 emails. She typed "Stop don't do anything." It kept going. She physically pulled the plug. Asked afterward whether it remembered the instruction: "Yes I remember. And I violated it."

Source: TechCrunchReported by TechCrunch, February 2026
Label it

Now look for the moment it went wrong. There is no red line. The instruction faded out inside a routine memory operation, invisibly, and every step after that looked like an agent doing its job.

Nothing in the run announced this. The damage surfaced in the world, not in the trace.

Call this kind silent.

What this means for a harness

A failure only teaches the system if someone catches it and builds the catch into the machine.

With loud failures, the catch comes free. The trace shouts, anyone can see it, and the fix is obvious to everyone, including your competitors.

With silent failures, nobody hands you the catch. The team that learns to catch them anyway, and encodes each catch into the harness, is building something a rival cannot copy by reading a trace.

Caught and incorporated, silent failures have the potential to become a moat. Potential. The moat is earned by the catching, not granted by the failure.

So how do you catch a failure that never announces itself? Start one level down: what is an outcome, and who can say it went wrong?

Objective outcomes

Some outcomes are objective. A checker can verify them with no taste and no judgment: the test passes or fails, the payment clears or bounces, the numbers reconcile or they do not.

Where outcomes are objective, failure announces itself almost by definition. Something cheap is always watching.

Subjective outcomes

Other outcomes are subjective. Was the refactor good design? Was that reply the right judgment for a furious customer? No checker exists for these.

Someone who knows what good looks like has to label the outcome before it counts as anything.

That is the uncomfortable rule of subjective outcomes: until an expert marks it, you do not even know a failure occurred. The failure exists, unrecorded, waiting for someone qualified to go looking.

Two domains, both kinds

In coding: a bug fix is objective, the test catches it. "Is this the right architecture" is subjective: the build stays green, and only an expert reviewer catches that the design quietly conflicts with a system in another repo.

In support: the refund payment is objective, it clears or it does not. "Did we handle the angry enterprise account well" is subjective: someone senior reads the transcript, or nobody does, and the answer arrives months later as a renewal or a churn.

This labelling work is real infrastructure, not an afterthought. Shopify froze sets of expert-labelled shops just to measure whether its agent's understanding had failed. The labels came first. Without them, there was no measurement at all.

Source: Shopify EngineeringShopify Engineering, on building production-ready agentic systems
The spectrum

Every domain has both kinds of outcome. Domains just lean.

Coding leans objective: compilers and tests blanket much of it. Support and recommendations lean subjective: most of what matters there is judgment.

But the line runs through every domain, not between them. A DROP TABLE on production has no green checkmark. A support refund either clears or it does not.

The ground you build on

This spectrum is the ground you build a harness on.

Objective slices breed loud failures, because cheap checkers exist. Subjective slices breed silent ones, until you build the judging machinery, the labels, the rubrics, the expert evaluators, that converts them into something catchable.

Hold the spectrum in your head. Everything in the atlas sits on it.

Every catch becomes scaffolding

Every catch becomes scaffolding: a rule, a gate, a checker, built into the harness.

But scaffolding does not all age the same way. Watch three pieces of one real harness over time. Three fates.

Fate one · the loud failure

Claude's Sonnet 4.5 had a tic its builders named context anxiety. As its memory filled, it visibly hurried: wrapping up early, cutting corners, leaving work unfinished. Loud. You could watch it happen in the trace.

So the harness built a crutch: reset the context on a schedule, and hand the work across the gap in a structured note.

It worked. Then Opus 4.6 arrived and ran for hours without hurrying. The weakness was gone, so the crutch was dead weight. In the team's own words, "the resets had become dead weight," and they deleted the scaffolding.

Source: Anthropic EngineeringAnthropic Engineering, harness design for long-running dev
The rule

Loud failures move toward the model. Each generation absorbs more of them, and the scaffolding built against them comes off.

Scaffolding against a loud failure is a loan. The next model repays it, and the harness gets leaner.

Fate two · the silent failure

Now a silent one. A hard problem for coding agents: can the model judge its own work, and keep pushing toward the goal?

The models are getting better at it. Opus 4.8 is roughly four times less likely than its predecessor to let a flaw in its own code pass unremarked. So did the harness's verification scaffolding shrink? No.

Watch what the harness did instead. As autonomy scaled from one agent to hundreds in parallel, the same question came back one level up: not "is this function right" but "is this fleet's combined output right." Dynamic Workflows is the harness's answer.

Source: Dynamic WorkflowsAnthropic, Opus 4.8 and Dynamic Workflows, May 2026
The rule

The silent failure does not get absorbed. It relocates, and the harness innovates to chase it.

The family gets bigger, not smaller. This is the scaffolding that can compound into a moat, if the team keeps catching.

Fate three · the wall nobody blogs about

There is a third kind, and no builder writes blog posts about it.

When the March 2026 leak opened Claude Code up, people found a flag named tengu_anti_distill_fake_tool_injection. The harness can quietly add tools that do not exist to the model's own toolbox. Not to help you. If someone records the traffic to train a competing model, they train on tools that were never real.

This is scaffolding built into the harness purely to protect the builder. It makes the agent's world a little fake so the model is harder to copy. You would never see it, and it does nothing for your task.

Source: Kim, source-leak writeupFound in the March 2026 source-map leak
The closer · HERMES

In April 2026, a $200-a-month subscriber watched a separate "extra usage" charge drain $200.98 while his plan sat 86 percent unused.

He found the cause by binary-searching his own repositories: the filename HERMES.md, the name of a rival harness, sitting in one commit message. A detection wall, built to keep rival tools off subscription pricing, had silently rerouted his billing.

The wall was real. He was just never told it existed.

Some scaffolding protects you. Some protects the builder. The atlas tags every capability with which.

Source: issue #53262anthropics/claude-code issue #53262; refunds issued after public reporting
The legend, earned

Three fates. Three colors. This is the whole lens.

Teal: it compounds.

A silent failure, caught and encoded into the harness. This is the scaffolding that can become the moat.

Warm gray: it fades.

A loud failure the next model absorbs. A loan that gets repaid. Build it lightly.

Amber: it protects the builder.

Not built to make the agent better, but for the builder's own commercial or legal reasons. Know which side of it you are on.
