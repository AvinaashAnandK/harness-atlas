A model is not an agent.
The harness makes it operational.

Treat the model as a broadly capable new hire. It is strong at general reasoning, but unfamiliar with your systems, your policies, and your history. The harness is the scaffolding you build around the model. It supplies the parts the model cannot reliably provide for itself.

The mental model

A harness does four jobs on every run.

  1. OrientSupply the right world and approach.
  2. ActControl what can touch real systems.
  3. RememberRestore the relevant history.
  4. ImproveTurn failures into reusable fixes.

Limitations and guardrails apply across the whole loop. Some protect the operator, some protect the builder, and some are hard ceilings.

A lens, not a law

Which harness investments survive a better model?

The fate labels are investment hypotheses. Visible, measurable failures may need less bespoke scaffolding as models improve. Silent failures tend to persist, and so do enforcement boundaries and business constraints.

The takeaway

The roadmap question is not “How smart is the model?”

Ask where a failure would surface. Ask how much damage it would cause. Then ask whether a better model removes the need for the fix or just makes the failure harder to notice.
