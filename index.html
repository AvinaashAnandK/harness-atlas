An atlas of agentic harnesses

The Harness Atlas

In 2026 the moat is not the model. It is the scaffolding around it. And not all of that scaffolding is worth the same. Some of it compounds and can become the moat. Some of it fades as models improve. And some of it is there to protect the company that builds the agent, not to make the agent better.

What this is

This atlas collects three things. First, the capabilities every serious harness ends up building. Second, the real failures that force each one into existence. Third, which of these investments hold their value over time.

It is organized the way the harness itself is organized. There are five layers, from how the agent gets oriented to the hard walls around it. There are twenty-four capabilities, and each one is a question the agent has to answer. And there are three fates, because scaffolding ages in three ways.

The difference is the kind of failure each piece was built to catch. A loud failure shows up in the run, so a better model usually learns to avoid it and the scaffolding fades. A silent failure leaves no trace, so a better model cannot fix it and the scaffolding compounds. The third kind is not about the agent's work at all. It protects the company that builds the agent. Telling the three apart is what this atlas is for.

The living atlas

The atlas starts with one example, Claude Code. The March 31, 2026 source-map leak exposed about 1,900 files and 512,000 lines of it, and many releases followed. From there the atlas grows. I keep adding capabilities seen in other harnesses, including Cursor, Replit, OpenClaw, Shopify's agents, Intercom Fin, Atlassian, and Google's agent stack, plus whatever ships next.

Claude Code is the first example, not the subject.

5 layers· 24 capabilities· 147 documented incidents

Every failure in this atlas is a real incident with a citation. None of them are hypothetical. Where I could not verify a claim against a primary source, I say so.
